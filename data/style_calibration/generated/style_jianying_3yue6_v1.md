# Style: 剪映导入-3月6日 v1

## Scope

This style applies to vertical talking-head parenting/education opinion videos where one speaker explains a child-related conflict, reframes the parent's reaction, and gives a practical takeaway.

Status: `calibrating`.

Confidence: medium for this single reference video, low for Angel's broader creator style.

## Overall Feel

The style is not purely serious. It is a口播 teaching video with light-variety emphasis. Speech remains primary, but the edit adds frequent small emotional cues so the viewer can feel confusion, correction, tension, hurt, insight, and completion.

The energy pattern is: hook quickly, add small humorous or dramatic markers, intensify around the conflict, then use success/idea/completion cues as the explanation becomes actionable.

## Editing Rules

| Rule ID | Rule |
|---|---|
| R1 | Keep the speaker and message primary. Effects should mark meaning, not cover speech. |
| R2 | Add an early framing/attention effect near the opening hook when the topic is introduced. |
| R3 | When the speaker expresses confusion, rhetorical questioning, or "why is this happening", use a short question/confusion sound cue. |
| R4 | When an incorrect assumption or poor reaction is being flagged, use an error/negative cue. |
| R5 | When the speaker gives a better reframe or correct response, use a short correct/success cue. |
| R6 | During recalled scenes, examples, or quoted child/parent interactions, use tape/retro/focus effects to signal example mode. |
| R7 | When the emotional or social conflict becomes serious, use tension, shock, glitch, or heartbeat-like cues. |
| R8 | When the child's emotional hurt is named, use a short sad/broken-heart family cue. |
| R9 | In practical advice segments, restart visual energy with urgency, spotlight, or tape-style emphasis. |
| R10 | Near the conclusion, use idea/success/completion cues to make the takeaway feel resolved. |
| R11 | Use small stickers/overlays as brief emphasis markers on hooks, realizations, instructions, and ending beats. |
| R12 | Keep subtitles continuous and sentence-level. Do not fragment every word. |

## Effect Vocabulary

| Family | Use |
|---|---|
| framing | Opening hook or attention setup |
| focus | Speaker authority, emotional face, final takeaway |
| tape/retro | Recalled event, example, quoted interaction |
| glitch | Hidden conflict, social harm, cognitive disruption |
| spotlight | Key instruction or important insight |
| urgency/sprint | Practical step that should feel active |

## Audio Vocabulary

| Family | Use |
|---|---|
| question | Confusion, rhetorical question, "why" moment |
| error/negative | Wrong assumption or problematic reaction |
| correct/success | Better response or actionable step |
| shock | Sudden realization or social seriousness |
| tension | Escalation, hidden conflict, uncomfortable truth |
| sad/broken | Child's hurt or emotional consequence |
| variety beat | Keeps explanation lively between dense points |
| idea/completion | Final useful idea or completed takeaway |

## Subtitle Rules

Use continuous orange sentence-level subtitles. The viewer should be able to follow the explanation even with sound off. Subtitle density should be high, but segmentation should follow complete thoughts rather than word-by-word fragments.

## Pacing Rules

Use frequent but short emphasis events. A reasonable first target is one emphasis cue every 8-12 seconds during dense explanation, with quieter gaps when the speaker needs authority or empathy. Avoid stacking more than two strong cues on the same moment unless it is a central turning point.

## Anti-Rules

| Anti-Rule ID | Anti-Rule |
|---|---|
| A1 | Do not place effects at fixed timestamps copied from the reference. |
| A2 | Do not use every available effect. The style is light-variety, not maximalist. |
| A3 | Do not add comedy cues when the child's hurt is being centered. |
| A4 | Do not break subtitles into word-level fragments. |
